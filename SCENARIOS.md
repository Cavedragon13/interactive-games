## Overview

The Survival Decision Series is an **evidence-based learning tool** that teaches critical thinking, priority assessment, and decision-making under pressure. Scenarios are based on real survival protocols from organizations like NASA, U.S. Coast Guard, Forest Service, and military training programs.

### Learning Objectives

Students who complete scenarios will:
1. **Analyze complex situations** and identify critical priorities
2. **Apply survival science** (e.g., "Rule of 3s": 3 minutes without air, 3 hours without shelter in harsh conditions, 3 days without water, 3 weeks without food)
3. **Compare their intuition** against expert protocols
4. **Learn from mistakes** through immediate feedback
5. **Develop metacognitive skills** by reflecting on their reasoning

### Scenario Structure

Each scenario consists of:
- **Emergency context** (200-400 words) - Sets the scene
- **10 survival items** - Player must rank them 1-10
- **Expert rankings** - Based on survival protocols
- **Expert reasoning** - Educational explanations for each item
- **Scoring thresholds** - Assessment levels (excellent/good/fair/poor)

## Scenario Design Principles

### 1. Authenticity
✅ **DO**: Base scenarios on real emergencies and expert protocols
❌ **DON'T**: Create fictional or sensationalized situations

**Good:** "Based on NASA lunar module emergency procedures"
**Bad:** "Stranded on alien planet with futuristic gadgets"

### 2. Educational Value
Every item should teach something about survival priorities.

**Example:** Why is a signal mirror ranked higher than a knife?
- Mirror: Can be seen 10+ miles away by aircraft, critical for rescue
- Knife: Useful for many tasks but doesn't directly facilitate rescue

### 3. Cognitive Challenge
Include "trap items" that seem important but aren't priorities.

**Example:** In desert survival:
- **Trap:** Canned food (seems useful, but digestion requires water)
- **Priority:** Signal mirror (rescue is critical before food matters)

### 4. Clear Difficulty Progression
Make rankings debatable but logical:
- **Items 1-3**: Clearly critical (oxygen, water, shelter)
- **Items 4-7**: Important but order is debatable
- **Items 8-10**: Nice-to-have or counterproductive

### 5. Cultural Sensitivity
Avoid scenarios that could traumatize or alienate students:
- ❌ Terrorism, warfare, violence
- ❌ Medical emergencies requiring graphic descriptions
- ✅ Natural disasters, wilderness survival, space emergencies

## Step-by-Step Creation Process

### Phase 1: Research (2-4 hours)

**1. Choose your emergency type:**
   - **Wilderness**: Desert, jungle, arctic, mountain
   - **Maritime**: Shipwreck, raft survival, island castaway
   - **Space**: Moon, Mars, space station emergency
   - **Industrial**: Mine collapse, oil rig disaster
   - **Natural disaster**: Earthquake, hurricane, wildfire evacuation

**2. Find expert sources:**
   - Government agencies (FEMA, NOAA, Forest Service)
   - Military manuals (U.S. Army FM 21-76 Survival Manual)
   - Professional organizations (NOLS, Red Cross, SAR teams)
   - Academic research (university emergency preparedness)

**3. Identify survival priorities:**
   - What kills you first? (oxygen, temperature, water, food)
   - What facilitates rescue? (signaling, communication)
   - What prevents injury/illness? (first aid, shelter)
   - What provides long-term sustainability? (food, tools)

### Phase 2: Item Selection (1-2 hours)

**You need exactly 10 items.** Use this formula:

**3 critical items** (ranks 1-3):
- Directly prevent death in hours/days
- Examples: Oxygen, water, emergency shelter, life raft

**4 important items** (ranks 4-7):
- Significantly improve survival odds
- Examples: Signal mirror, first aid, fire starter, rope

**2 useful items** (ranks 8-9):
- Provide comfort or minor advantages
- Examples: Knife, compass, sunglasses, hat

**1 least useful item** (rank 10):
- Lowest priority or potentially counterproductive
- Examples: Books, playing cards, cosmetics, non-essential food

### Phase 3: Expert Ranking Logic (1-2 hours)

Use the **Survival Priority Hierarchy**:

```
1. IMMEDIATE LIFE SUPPORT (minutes to hours)
   ↓ Oxygen, critical medical, extreme cold/heat protection

2. SHORT-TERM SURVIVAL (hours to days)
   ↓ Water, shelter, signaling for rescue

3. MEDIUM-TERM NEEDS (days to weeks)
   ↓ Food, first aid, fire/warmth

4. LONG-TERM SUSTAINABILITY (weeks+)
   ↓ Tools, navigation, clothing

5. COMFORT / PSYCHOLOGICAL
   ↓ Entertainment, non-essentials
```

**Apply the "What kills you first?" test:**
- Will lack of this item kill you in **minutes**? → Rank 1-2
- Will it kill you in **hours**? → Rank 2-4
- Will it kill you in **days**? → Rank 4-6
- Will it kill you in **weeks**? → Rank 7-10

### Phase 4: Write Reasoning (2-3 hours)

For each item, explain:
1. **What it does** (functional description)
2. **Why it matters** (survival benefit)
3. **When it's critical** (time sensitivity)
4. **Trade-offs** (why it's ranked higher/lower than alternatives)

**Example (Good Reasoning):**
```
Item: Water Purification Tablets
Rank: 2
Reasoning: "In wilderness survival, dehydration is the second-greatest killer after
hypothermia. Humans can survive only 3 days without water. While finding water sources
is possible, drinking untreated water risks dysentery, which accelerates dehydration
and can be fatal within days. Purification tablets weigh ounces, purify gallons, and
prevent the most common cause of death in extended wilderness survival. They rank
just below shelter because hypothermia can kill in hours, while dehydration takes days."
```

**Example (Weak Reasoning):**
```
Item: Knife
Rank: 7
Reasoning: "Knives are useful for cutting things."
```

### Phase 5: Scenario Context Writing (1-2 hours)

Write a 3-4 paragraph emergency description:

**Paragraph 1: The Emergency**
- What happened? (plane crash, ship sinking, vehicle breakdown)
- Where are you? (location, terrain, climate)
- What's your immediate situation? (injuries, time of day, weather)

**Paragraph 2: Environmental Threats**
- What will kill you if you don't act? (temperature, water, predators)
- What resources are unavailable? (rescue timeline, communication)
- What makes this environment unique?

**Paragraph 3: The Challenge**
- Explain the ranking task
- Mention the expert source (e.g., "Based on U.S. Coast Guard protocols")
- Set expectations (e.g., "Lower scores indicate better alignment with proven strategies")

**Example Context:**
```
ARCTIC RESEARCH STATION EMERGENCY

A severe blizzard has damaged your arctic research station 200 miles from the nearest
settlement. Radio equipment is destroyed, and the storm has grounded all aircraft for
at least 5 days. Outside temperature is -40°F (-40°C) with 50 mph winds, creating a
wind chill of -80°F. Three of your team's heated modules are intact, but fuel reserves
are limited. You have 10 items salvaged from the wreckage.

Arctic survival is a race against two killers: hypothermia and dehydration. At -40°F,
exposed skin freezes in minutes. Wind chill can drop effective temperature to -80°F,
where death from exposure occurs in under 30 minutes without proper shelter.
Dehydration is accelerated by cold, dry air—you lose moisture with every breath.
Rescue depends on weather clearing, which could take 5-7 days.

Your task: Rank these 10 items in order of survival importance (1 = most critical,
10 = least critical). Your ranking will be compared against protocols used by polar
research stations and military cold-weather survival training. Lower scores indicate
better alignment with expert priorities.
```

Choose 6 colors that match your environment:

- **Accent Primary**: Highlights, buttons, interactive elements
- **Accent Secondary**: Secondary highlights

### Phase 7: Scoring Thresholds (15 minutes)

Set assessment thresholds based on difficulty:

**Standard Thresholds:**
- **Excellent**: ≤15 difference points (player deeply understands priorities)
- **Good**: ≤30 difference points (solid understanding)
- **Fair**: ≤50 difference points (basic grasp but significant gaps)
- **Poor**: >50 difference points (fundamental misunderstandings)

**Easier Scenarios** (fewer debatable rankings):
- Excellent: ≤20
- Good: ≤35
- Fair: ≤55

**Harder Scenarios** (many items close in priority):
- Excellent: ≤12
- Good: ≤25
- Fair: ≤45

## Item Selection Strategies

### The "Trap Item" Technique

Include 1-2 items that *seem* important but aren't priorities:

**Example: Desert Survival**
- **Trap**: Canned food (students think "I need energy!")
- **Reality**: Food digestion requires water, worsening dehydration
- **Learning**: In hot environments, water >>> food

**Example: Lunar Survival**
- **Trap**: Pistol (students think "self-defense!")
- **Reality**: No threats on moon, adds weight, useless for survival
- **Learning**: Earth survival instincts don't apply in space

### The "Context Matters" Principle

Same item, different ranking depending on scenario:

**Knife:**
- **Jungle**: Rank 3-4 (machete functionality, critical for shelter)
- **Moon**: Rank 9-10 (no wood to cut, minimal utility)
- **Ocean**: Rank 6-7 (useful for rope/fishing, but not top priority)

### The "Multi-Purpose" Factor

Items with multiple uses rank higher:

**Signal Mirror:**
- Primary: Visual signaling (10+ mile range)
- Secondary: Start fires (focus sunlight)
- Tertiary: Personal hygiene (morale)
→ Ranks 2-4 in most scenarios

**Rope:**
- Primary: Secure shelter
- Secondary: Create rescue signals (large X pattern)
- Tertiary: Tie equipment, make splints
→ Ranks 5-8 depending on scenario

## Expert Ranking Logic

### Decision Framework

For each item, ask:

1. **Time Sensitivity**: How quickly does lack of this item kill you?
   - Minutes → Rank 1-2
   - Hours → Rank 2-4
   - Days → Rank 4-7
   - Weeks → Rank 7-10

2. **Rescue Facilitation**: Does this directly help you get rescued?
   - Yes, dramatically (radio, flare) → Rank 1-3
   - Yes, moderately (mirror, whistle) → Rank 3-5
   - No → Rank based on survival value

3. **Replaceability**: Can you improvise a substitute?
   - No substitute (oxygen, medicine) → Rank higher
   - Improvise possible (knife, rope) → Rank lower

4. **Impact Multiplier**: Does this enable other survival strategies?
   - Yes (fire starter enables warmth + signaling + water purification) → Rank higher
   - No (single-purpose item) → Rank lower

### Example Ranking Process

**Scenario:** Mountain plane crash in winter

**Item:** Fire Starter Kit
1. **Time Sensitivity**: Hypothermia kills in 3-6 hours at 20°F → HIGH
2. **Rescue Facilitation**: Smoke visible for miles → MODERATE
3. **Replaceability**: Very difficult without tools → LOW
4. **Impact Multiplier**: Enables warmth, signaling, water purification, cooking → HIGH

**Conclusion:** Rank 2-3 (just below shelter/insulation)

---

**Item:** Canned Food
1. **Time Sensitivity**: Starvation takes 3+ weeks → LOW
2. **Rescue Facilitation**: None → NONE
3. **Replaceability**: Humans can survive without food → HIGH
4. **Impact Multiplier**: None, actually requires water for digestion → NEGATIVE

**Conclusion:** Rank 9-10

## Educational Frameworks

### Bloom's Taxonomy Alignment

This game addresses multiple cognitive levels:

**Level 3 - Applying**: Students apply survival principles to rank items
**Level 4 - Analyzing**: Students compare their reasoning to expert protocols
**Level 5 - Evaluating**: Students justify their rankings and critique alternatives
**Level 6 - Creating**: (Extension) Students design their own scenarios

### Classroom Discussion Prompts

After students complete a scenario, use these questions:

1. **Metacognitive**: "What surprised you about the expert rankings?"
2. **Analytical**: "Why did experts rank [item] higher than [item]?"
3. **Application**: "How would rankings change if [scenario variable changed]?"
4. **Evaluation**: "Do you agree with the expert reasoning? Why or why not?"

### Assessment Rubric

| Score Range | Grade | Understanding Level |
|-------------|-------|---------------------|
| 0-15 | A | Exceptional - Expert-level prioritization |
| 16-30 | B | Proficient - Strong survival reasoning |
| 31-50 | C | Adequate - Basic understanding with gaps |
| 51-70 | D | Developing - Significant misunderstandings |
| 71+ | F | Beginning - Fundamental misconceptions |

### Scenario Atmosphere

Use colors, icons, and language to create immersion:

**Desert Scenario:**
- Colors: Warm oranges, browns, yellows
- Icons: ☀️ 🏜️ 🦎
- Language: "Scorching," "relentless sun," "parched"

**Ocean Scenario:**
- Colors: Deep blues, teals, white foam
- Icons: 🌊 ⛵ 🐟
- Language: "Vast," "rolling waves," "horizon"

**Space Scenario:**
- Colors: Black, silver, purple accents
- Icons: 🌙 🚀 🛰️
- Language: "Void," "weightless," "hostile vacuum"

### Accessibility Considerations

- **Text contrast**: Minimum 4.5:1 ratio (WCAG AA)
- **Font size**: 16px minimum for body text
- **Color blindness**: Don't rely on color alone (use icons + text)
- **Screen readers**: All item descriptions should be self-explanatory

## Testing and Validation

### Playtesting Checklist

Before using in classroom, test with 3-5 people:

- [ ] All 10 items have clear descriptions
- [ ] Expert reasoning makes sense to non-experts
- [ ] Scoring thresholds feel fair (not too easy/hard)
- [ ] Theme colors are readable and immersive
- [ ] No typos or grammatical errors
- [ ] Scenario context is engaging but not traumatic
- [ ] Rankings are debatable but justifiable

### Student Feedback Cycle

After first classroom use:
1. **Survey students**: What was confusing? What was interesting?
2. **Analyze scores**: Are too many students getting "Poor"? Adjust thresholds.
3. **Review reasoning**: Do expert explanations answer student questions?
4. **Iterate**: Update scenario based on feedback

## Classroom Implementation

### Pre-Activity (10 minutes)
1. **Context setting**: Show real-world example (NASA survival training video)
2. **Survival science**: Teach "Rule of 3s" (3 min/3 hr/3 day/3 week)
3. **Expectations**: Explain this is about learning, not "winning"

### Activity (15-20 minutes)
1. **Individual work**: Students rank items independently
2. **No collaboration**: Prevent groupthink
3. **Reflection**: Students write 1-2 sentences justifying their top 3 items

### Post-Activity Discussion (15-20 minutes)
1. **Share scores**: Ask for volunteers to share (no shame for low scores!)
2. **Debate**: Pick 2-3 controversial items, have students defend rankings
3. **Expert reveal**: Show expert reasoning for most debated items
4. **Connect to curriculum**: How does this apply to [science/history/geography]?

### Extensions

**For Advanced Students:**
- Design your own scenario (provide template)
- Research alternative survival strategies (bushcraft vs. modern gear)
- Analyze real survival stories (compare to game logic)

**For English Language Learners:**
- Pre-teach vocabulary (hypothermia, dehydration, signaling)
- Pair with buddy for discussion
- Provide visual aids for each item

### Mini-Scenario: Home Fire Evacuation

**Educational Use:** Middle school fire safety unit

**10 Items to Rank:**
1. House keys (rank 8) - Can't re-enter during fire
2. Fire extinguisher (rank 7) - Only if fire is small; evacuate first
3. Phone (rank 2) - Call 911 immediately
4. Shoes (rank 3) - Protect feet from glass/debris
5. Jacket (rank 5) - Warmth while waiting outside
6. Photo albums (rank 10) - Sentimental but not survival-critical
7. Pets (rank 1) - Living beings, critical to rescue
8. Flashlight (rank 4) - Navigate smoke-filled rooms
9. Wallet (rank 6) - ID helpful but not immediate priority
10. Laptop (rank 9) - Replaceable, low priority

**Learning Goals:**
- "Get out, stay out" principle (don't return for items)
- Call emergency services immediately
- Living beings are top priority
- Practice makes evacuations automatic
